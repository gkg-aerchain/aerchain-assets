Key Findings from Preliminary Research
1

High-volume, multi-format retail operation: Barnes & Noble runs ~600+ bookstores across all 50 U.S. states and sells ~190M physical books per year, plus gifts, games, stationery, and digital content, implying a very large and diversified supplier base and high PO/invoice volumes for both direct and indirect spend.

2

Mature vendor and supply chain governance: B&N publishes detailed vendor operational guidelines, EDI standards and a vendor portal for merchandise suppliers and supply-chain partners, indicating structured processes for onboarding, compliance, transportation, and inventory flow.

3

Strategic focus on supply chain excellence: Appointment of a Chief Supply Chain Officer and the emphasis on supply chain performance as “central” to growth suggest procurement and logistics are now treated as strategic levers rather than purely transactional functions.

4

Localized, bottom-up buying model (“cluster system”): Under Elliott and CEO James Daunt, B&N has moved to a store-cluster model where local teams have greater autonomy on assortment, requiring procurement and merchandising processes that can support decentralized decision-making while still aggregating supplier negotiations.

5

Significant untapped value in addressable indirect spend: B&N’s ~$600M addressable indirect spend (~17% of revenue, in line with retail benchmarks of 10–18%) is fragmented across ~1,200 active suppliers and managed by a lean 4-person procurement team — a structural setup where 3–6% savings ($18M–$36M annually) and 60–70% transaction automation are realistic without a rip-and-replace.

02
Assumed Current State Challenges
Assumed pain points based on preliminary research
⏰
Indirect / GNFR spend visibility and control

Fragmented ownership of indirect categories (marketing, facilities, IT, logistics, professional services) across central functions and 600+ stores.

Legacy manual processes and maverick spend typical in retail GNFR mean many low-value invoices and non-PO payments bypass procurement.

Limited consolidated view of contracts and rates for services such as cleaning, maintenance, and local marketing.

Impact: Higher unit costs, leakage versus negotiated contracts, and difficulty capturing 5–15% achievable savings in GNFR.
⏰
Tail spend and store-level purchasing

Large number of low-value, high-volume store purchases (supplies, local services, events) tend to sit in the tail with weak process discipline.

Empowered local managers under the Daunt model may increase non-standard, non-PO and P-card purchases if not guided by easy-to-use catalogs and self-service channels.

Limited ability to leverage data on tail suppliers for consolidation or negotiation.

Impact: Process cost dominates item value, poor compliance, and missed aggregation opportunities across 600+ locations.
⏰
Logistics, distribution, and omnichannel fulfillment

Need for fast, reliable replenishment to 600+ stores plus e-commerce distribution centers, especially for bestsellers and seasonal items.

Rising transportation and labor costs in the logistics network.

Requirement to support bulk orders, BOPIS and potential future marketplaces within tight SLAs.

Impact: Higher total landed cost, potential stockouts or overstocks, and pressure on service levels if logistics contracts and performance are not tightly managed by procurement and supply chain.
⏰
Geopolitical, ESG, and community expectations

Exposure to global paper, printing, and manufacturing supply chains subject to geopolitical, environmental, and cost shocks.

Public and local-community scrutiny as B&N expands into neighborhoods with strong independent bookstore cultures.

Growing ESG expectations around diversity of suppliers, sustainable sourcing, and labor practices in the extended supply chain.

Impact: Reputational risk, supply disruption, and potential cost spikes if ESG and geopolitical risk are not embedded into sourcing strategy and supplier selection.
